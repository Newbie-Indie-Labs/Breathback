What Your AC

Not just electricity. Not just bills. Your refrigerant-based cooling system is actively participating in one of the slowest, most catastrophic processes on Earth.

8x

India's Cooling Demand Will Multiply

India's refrigerant-based cooling demand is projected to grow 8 times over — from 2017–18 levels to over 950 million tons of refrigeration by 2037–38. This will drive a 6.5x rise in refrigerant demand. Every new AC installed without a plan is a future emission locked in. Source: India Cooling Action Plan (ICAP), MoEFCC, 2019

When the Ozone Layer Can Fully Recover

That's the earliest scientists predict full stratospheric ozone recovery — only if nations act immediately and completely. A child born today in Rajasthan or Tamil Nadu will be middle-aged before the sky heals from what our cooling habits have already done. Source: UNEP RTOC Assessment Report, 2022

Only 8%

Indian Homes Have AC — For Now

As of 2018, only about 8% of Indian households had an air conditioner. The Bureau of Energy Efficiency (BEE) projects this will reach 40% by 2038. That's hundreds of millions of new refrigerant-filled units entering homes across the subcontinent in under 15 years. The window to shape that transition is right now. Source: BEE India / Down to Earth, 2025

Of Warming the Kigali Amendment Can Prevent

The Kigali Amendment — which India ratified in 2021 — could avoid 0.5°C of global warming by 2100 by phasing down HFC refrigerants. But the amendment alone isn't enough. It needs millions of Indian families to understand why, and choose differently, before it's mandated. Source: NRDC India / Kigali Amendment, Montreal Protocol

Most Indian families installed air conditioning without being told the full picture. It's not just electricity costs. Every old R-22 or R-410A unit you run leaks potent greenhouse chemicals silently into the atmosphere.

India's AC service sector alone accounts for roughly 40% of all refrigerant consumption in the country — meaning the maintenance and repair of existing units is as much a problem as buying new ones (CEEW, 2023). And only about 36% of AC technicians across India have received formal training in safe refrigerant handling (CEEW primary survey, New Delhi / Jaipur / Madurai).

You didn't cause this problem intentionally. But now that you know, you have the power to be part of ending it.

India's Own Plan Confirms This

The India Cooling Action Plan (ICAP, 2019) — the world's first national cooling strategy — targets a 20–40% reduction in cooling-related energy use and training for over 100,000 service technicians in alternative refrigerants by 2038. The government knows. What's missing is household-level awareness and action.

These aren't ancient ideas — they're intelligent engineering that worked for thousands of years and works even better today when combined with modern materials.

🏺

Terracotta Pot Cooler

Two clay pots, wet sand in between, and a damp cloth on top. Evaporative cooling drops temperature by 8–15°C with zero electricity, zero refrigerant. Used across North Africa and India for centuries.

✓ 0 electricity · 0 emissions · ~₹400 to build
💨

Cross-Ventilation Design

Opening windows on opposite walls creates a pressure differential that pulls cool air through naturally. Adding a wet curtain at the inlet lowers incoming air temperature by 5–8°C.

✓ Works best in Indian climate · Zero cost
🌿

Rooftop Garden / Green Roof

A layer of soil and plants on your roof reduces indoor temperature by 3–7°C by blocking solar radiation and releasing moisture. Bonus: grows food, manages rainwater, reduces noise.

✓ Cuts AC use by up to 50% in top-floor homes
🪨

White / Cool Roof Paint

Coating your roof white reflects 80–90% of sunlight instead of absorbing it. This single change has been shown to reduce indoor temperatures by 4–6°C and cut cooling energy by 20–70%.

✓ ₹15–40 per sq.ft · Lasts 5–10 years
💧

Water-Body Microclimate

A small pond, fountain, or even a series of wide open water containers near windows cools surrounding air through evaporation. Traditional Indian homes used this as the primary cooling system.

✓ Creates 2–5°C local cooling zone outdoors
🌳

Strategic Tree Planting

Trees on the west and south sides block the most intense afternoon sun. Mature trees can cool a house by 3–8°C and reduce air conditioning energy use by 30%. Fruit trees give you food too.

✓ 10–15 year investment with lifelong returns

The World Is Watching — And Moving

Countries That
Already Chose Right

India isn't alone in this crisis — but others have moved faster. These are real, documented examples of nations and cities that chose natural and low-refrigerant cooling — and the results speak clearly.

🇮🇷
Iran — Yazd
Badgir Wind Towers · 12th century — present

The city of Yazd in Iran's central desert has used badgir (wind catchers) since the 12th century — tower structures that capture cooler high-altitude breezes and channel them underground into homes. These structures also cooled water reservoirs to near-freezing temperatures in desert summers. No electricity, no refrigerant, no emissions. The technology works through pure physics: pressure differentials and thermal mass. Architects in Masdar City (UAE) and the Eastgate Centre (Zimbabwe) have revived and modernised this exact approach.

Result: Zero-emission passive cooling in a desert climate comparable to Rajasthan. Still functioning after 800+ years. Source: France24, 2023 / Encyclopaedia Iranica
🇸🇬
Singapore
Green Mark Certification · Tropical Climate

Singapore — a tropical city-state with temperatures and humidity levels comparable to Chennai or Kolkata — has made passive cooling a mandatory consideration in building design through its Green Mark certification. The programme mandates natural ventilation assessments before mechanical cooling is even considered. Singapore is also a signatory to the Global Cooling Pledge and has publicly committed to sharing its technical expertise with developing nations.

Result: Tropical city successfully integrating passive cooling into dense urban construction — proving it's possible in conditions identical to coastal India. Source: ASEAN Centre for Energy, 2024 / UNEP Beat the Heat initiative
🇪🇸
Spain — Andalusia
Natural Ventilation in Public Buildings

Andalusia in southern Spain — a hot, semi-arid region with climate similarities to parts of Deccan India — has systematically retrofitted schools and public buildings with natural ventilation systems. In documented studies, combining natural ventilation with existing mechanical systems reduced annual primary energy usage by 18–33% across Andalusian schools. A separate analysis found that AC operating hours dropped by up to 29.5% where cross-ventilation was optimised.

Result: 18–33% annual energy reduction in public buildings; proven in a hot-dry climate. Source: Peer-reviewed, ScienceDirect — Natural Ventilation study, 2023
🇩🇰
Denmark
Passive House Natural Ventilation Standard

Denmark has built and monitored passive houses designed entirely around natural ventilation and thermal mass — no mechanical cooling. A study of the "Home for Life" dwelling in Vejle, Denmark found that thermal comfort (per European Standard EN 15251) was maintained in 80–90% of occupied hours using purely ventilative cooling. The same approach has been replicated in Germany and Austria with equivalent results.

Result: 80–90% of hours meeting thermal comfort standards — with zero refrigerant-based cooling. Source: International Journal of Ventilation, Foldbjerg et al., VELUX A/S Denmark
The India Parallel

India already has ancient equivalents of all these systems — the jaali (latticed stone screen), the stepwell microclimate, the mud-and-lime kachcha house, the courtyard with a tulsi plant. These weren't primitive — they were climate-engineered. The problem isn't a lack of knowledge. It's that we stopped teaching it, and started selling ACs instead. The world's best examples of natural cooling echo what Indian vernacular architecture already knew.
